### Narrative

Provenance Guard exposes three endpoints: a submission endpoint that runs text through two detection signals and returns a classification, an appeal endpoint that flags a prior classification for human review, and a log endpoint that returns recent audit entries. The two detection signals run independently in parallel — Signal 1 makes a Groq LLM call for semantic and stylistic analysis while Signal 2 runs a pure-Python stylometrics pass for structural analysis — and their scores are averaged into a single confidence value. No automated re-classification ever happens; the system surfaces uncertainty honestly and defers disputed cases to a human reviewer.

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: Groq LLM

Calls the Groq API with the submitted text and asks the model to evaluate whether the writing reads as AI-generated or human-written based on semantic and stylistic properties (word choice, sentence flow, coherence patterns, hedging language, etc.).

- **Output:** a raw float between 0 and 1 (1 = almost certainly AI, 0 = almost certainly human)
- **Strength:** captures high-level writing "feel" that structural rules miss
- **Weakness:** may flag polished or formal human writers (academics, lawyers, careful non-native speakers)

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics

Pure-Python analysis of structural properties of the text. No external API call.

Three features measured:
1. **Sentence length variance** — AI text tends toward uniform sentence lengths; high variance suggests human writing
2. **Type-token ratio (TTR)** — ratio of unique words to total words; low TTR can indicate repetitive AI phrasing
3. **Punctuation density** — AI text often uses punctuation in predictable patterns

- **Output:** a raw float between 0 and 1 (1 = structurally similar to AI, 0 = structurally similar to human)
- **Strength:** fast, deterministic, no API cost, no rate limits
- **Weakness:** beginner writers or non-native speakers with simple, uniform sentences may score high on AI-likelihood

### Score Aggregation

```
confidence = (signal_1_score + signal_2_score) / 2
```

Simple average. Both signals are treated as equally weighted.

## Uncertainty Representation

| Confidence Score | Label Category          | Meaning                                                   |
|-----------------|-------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|
| 0.85 and above  | High-confidence AI      | Strong evidence from both signals that text is AI-generated |
| 0.60 – 0.84     | Uncertain, leaning AI   | Some AI characteristics detected but not conclusive       |
| 0.40 – 0.59     | Uncertain, leaning Human| Some human characteristics detected but not conclusive    |
| Below 0.40      | High-confidence Human   | Strong evidence from both signals that text is human-written |

The thresholds are intentionally asymmetric. The system requires a score of 0.85 or higher before making a confident AI claim, but only requires a score below 0.40 to make a confident human claim. This protects human writers from false positives — it is worse to wrongly accuse a human of using AI than to miss an AI-generated submission.

---

## Transparency Labels

These are the exact strings the Label Generator returns. They are chosen based on the confidence score bucket above.

**High-confidence AI** (score ≥ 0.85):
> "This content was likely generated by AI. Confidence: High"

**Uncertain, leaning AI** (0.60 ≤ score < 0.85):
> "We were unable to determine with confidence whether this content was human or AI-generated, though it shows some characteristics of AI-generated content. If you believe this is incorrect, you may submit an appeal."

**Uncertain, leaning Human** (0.40 ≤ score < 0.60):
> "We were unable to determine with confidence whether this content was human or AI-generated, though it shows some characteristics of human writing. If you believe this is incorrect, you may submit an appeal."

**High-confidence Human** (score < 0.40):
> "This content appears to be human-written. Confidence: High"

## Appeals Workflow

1. A creator submits `POST /appeal` with their `content_id` and a `creator_reasoning` string explaining why they believe the classification is wrong.
2. The system looks up the content record by `content_id`.
3. The content's status field is updated from its current value to `"under_review"`.
4. The appeal — including the `content_id`, `creator_reasoning`, timestamp, and original classification — is written to the audit log.
5. The system returns a JSON confirmation: the `content_id`, updated status, and a message that the appeal has been received.
6. No automated re-classification occurs. A human reviewer reads the audit log entry and decides the outcome manually.

## Edge Cases

### Case 1: Polished Human Writer Flagged as AI (Groq Blind Spot)

**Scenario:** A law professor submits a carefully edited essay. The prose is precise, formal, and nearly free of grammatical variation — Signal 1 (Groq) interprets this "too-perfect" style as AI-generated and returns a high score.

**Risk:** A legitimate human writer receives an AI label or an uncertain-leaning-AI label.

**Mitigation in current design:** The asymmetric thresholds (requiring ≥ 0.85 for a confident AI label) reduce but do not eliminate this risk. The appeal workflow gives the writer a path to contest the result. Signal 2 (Stylometrics) may partially counteract a high Signal 1 score if the writer uses varied sentence structures.

### Case 2: Beginner Writer Flagged as AI (Stylometrics Blind Spot)

**Scenario:** A non-native English speaker writes short, simple, grammatically uniform sentences. Signal 2 (Stylometrics) detects low sentence-length variance and low type-token ratio — both patterns associated with AI output — and returns a high score.

**Risk:** A legitimate human writer is penalized for a natural feature of their writing style, not for using AI.

**Mitigation in current design:** Again, the high-confidence-AI threshold of 0.85 provides a buffer. Signal 1 (Groq) evaluates meaning and context, not just structure, and may score the same text as clearly human, pulling the average down. The appeal workflow is the primary recourse for this writer.

## AI Tool Plan

### M3: Submission Endpoint + Signal 1

**What to provide to the AI tool:**

The full planning.md — specifically the architecture narrative, the submission flow diagram, the Signal 1 description, the score aggregation formula, the four transparency label variants with exact string text, and the audit log field list. Also provided requirements.txt so it knew Flask, Groq, and python-dotenv were the only dependencies.

**What to ask it to generate:**

The complete Flask app skeleton: `app.py` with `/submit` and `/log` endpoints, `signals/groq_signal.py` that calls the Groq API and returns a `score` and `reasoning`, the `_load_log`/`_save_log` helpers for the JSON audit log, the `_get_attribution_and_label` function mapping confidence scores to the four label categories, and the audit log entry structure. Signal 2 was left as a `0.5` placeholder in `app.py`.

**How to verify the output:**

Ran the Flask server and submitted test text via Python `requests`. Checked that the response contained `content_id`, `attribution`, `confidence`, and `label`. Inspected `audit_log.json` to confirm all required fields were written. Discovered the initial output used human-readable attribution strings (`'Uncertain'`, `'Human-written'`) instead of the machine-readable codes specified in planning.md. Issued a follow-up prompt to correct this to the four exact values: `likely_ai`, `uncertain_leaning_ai`, `uncertain_leaning_human`, `likely_human`.

### M4: Signal 2 + Confidence Scoring

**What to provide to the AI tool:**

The Signal 2 section of planning.md (three metrics, exact normalization formulas, and the 0–1 score direction), the updated confidence formula `(0.6 * signal_1) + (0.4 * signal_2)`, and the current `app.py` showing exactly where the `SIGNAL_2_PLACEHOLDER = 0.5` lived and how it fed into the confidence calculation.

**What to ask it to generate:**

`signals/stylometric_signal.py` implementing sentence length variance, type-token ratio, and punctuation density in pure Python with no external libraries, returning a `{"score": float}` dict. Also asked it to remove the placeholder from `app.py`, wire in the real signal, update the confidence formula to the weighted average, and add a console `print` showing both signal scores on every submission.

**How to verify the output:**

Ran `test_signals.py` against four sample texts (clearly AI, clearly human, borderline human academic, borderline AI lightly edited). The initial test texts were too short — all four scores clustered between 0.63 and 0.66 with no meaningful separation. Overrode the test inputs with longer 150+ word texts that more strongly exercise each metric. The revised scores ranged from 0.51 (clearly human, high sentence-length variance and dense punctuation) to 0.71 (clearly AI, near-zero sentence-length variance and minimal punctuation), with the two borderline cases landing at 0.67.
